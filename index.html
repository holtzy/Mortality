Association between mortality-related health metrics and mental disorders




Systematic reviews have consistently demonstrated that those with mental disorders have an increased risk of premature mortality. We observed that people with mental disorders have 2-3 times higher mortality rates than those without mental disorders, and they experienced a life expectancy 7-10 shorter than persons from the general population. We have provided a comprehensive analysis of mortality associated with different types of mental disorders, presenting both Mortality Rate Ratios (MRRs) and premature mortality estimated using Life-Years-Lost (LYLs).




A study by O. Plana Ripoll et al.

Mortality rate & mental disorders


Difference between Mortality Rates and Mortality Rate Ratios

Mortality rate is defined as the number of people dying in a given population and time period. It is estimated as a count of individuals who died per number of people in the population, per unit of time.

Our study examined years of life for the entire Danish population, so the unit of measurement is person-years. We show the rates for those with a mental disorder, and those without mental disorders. As expected, for both groups, mortality rates increase constantly with age. However, the mortality rate for those with mental disorder is always higher than those without mental disorder.

The mortality rates for the two groups across the age groups shows a ‘bulge’ during young adulthood (20-40 years). By comparing the two rates, we can derive a ratio. The Mortality Rate Ratio is shown in the right-hand panel. It is not constant over age. Overall, Mortality Rate Ratio peaked around age 35 years old, when people with mental disorders have an 8-fold higher mortality rate, compared to those without mental disorders.

The left-hand panel shows age across the horizontal axis, while the horizontal axis shows the number of people who have died at different ages scaled to 100,000 persons-years, during the period of observation of our study. Note that the vertical axis is shown in a log scale.

Sex-specific and Cause of death-specific Mortality rates

Sex-specific mortality rate ratio for those with any mental disorder showed a similar pattern for males and females, although males had a higher Mortality Rate Ratio compared to females after age 20 years. Causes of death can also be split between natural and artifical cause of death. It appears that mortality rate ratio linked with unnatural cause of death are highly more frequent for people with mental disorders, notably because of the effect of suicide. The next part of this document allows to dig this concept further.


Mortality rate ratios by specific causes of death


Compared to people without mental disorders, people with mental disorders have between 2 and 3 times higher rate of dying during a period of observation (MRR=2.42 [95% CI 2.41-2.43]).

That is, people with mental disorder have premature mortality. Our new study provides details on this risk by types of mental disorders (e.g. people with depression, substance use disorders etc).

In addition, we can examine what was the underlying final cause of death (e.g. cancer, stroke, suicide etc).

For historical reasons, Causes of Death of often divided into broad categories. Under the overall category “Any Cause”, there are two broad subcategories: (a) “Unnatural Causes” which includes suicide, accidents and being the victim of homicide; and (b) “Natural Causes” which include causes like cancer (i.e. neoplasms), infectious diseases, and circulatory disorders).

Sex-specific MRR were systematically higher among males except for the association between any mental disorder and suicide that was higher for females (MRR=17·3) than for males. Last but not least, it is possible to split this analysis at the mental disorder level. For instance, it appears that substance-use is associated with very high MRRs, notably concerning unnatural causes of death and alcohol misuse. Life Year Lost

Excess in life year losts is another way to study the premature component of mortality. One sentence of explanation, how is it different from MRR. Being diagnosed with a mental disorder is almost always linked with life year lost, both for natural and unnatural cause of death. Substance use is the disorder the most associated with life year lost. Surprisingly, we found that people with all types of mental disorder had fewer LYLs associated with neoplasm-related deaths compared to the general population.
