Association between mortality-related health metrics and mental disorders




Systematic reviews have consistently demonstrated that those with mental disorders have an increased risk of premature mortality. We provide a comprehensive analysis of mortality by different types of mental disorders, presenting both mortality rate ratios and premature mortality based on Life Years Lost




A study by O. Plana Ripoll et al.

﹀

Mortality rate & mental disorders

Mortality rate is defined as the proportion of people dying. As expected, mortality rate increases constantly with age (Left). Moreover, mortality rate of those with mental disorder is always higher than the general population. This difference can be quantified through the mortality rate ratio. It is not constant over age. Overall, mortality rate ratio peaked around age 35 years old, when people with mental disorders have a 8 fold increase of mortality.

Sex-specific mortality rate ratio for those with any mental disorder showed a similar trend for males and females, although males had a higher ratio than females after age 20 years.

Mortality rates by specific causes of death

People with mental disorders have an increase in death probability, and so for any cause of death. The cause of death specific mortality rate ratios range from 1.4 for neoplasm to 11.8 for suicide. Cause-specific MRR were systematically higher among males; however, the association between any mental disorder and suicide was higher for females (MRR=17·3 [16·1-18·6]) than for males.

Excess in Life Year Lost

Excess in life year losts is another way to study the premature component of mortality

Surprisingly, we found that men with all types of mental disorder had fewer LYLs associated with neoplasm-related deaths compared to the general population.
